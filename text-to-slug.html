FAQ
Common Questions
A URL slug is the human-readable part of a web address that identifies a specific page. For example, in example.com/blog/my-first-post, the slug is my-first-post. Good slugs are short, lowercase, use hyphens as separators, and contain relevant keywords.
Google treats hyphens as word separators, making individual keywords recognisable for SEO. Underscores join words together, so "my_post" is treated as one word, not two. Unless your platform requires underscores (e.g. Python file names), hyphens are the recommended choice for URLs.
Stop words are common words like "a", "the", "and", "in", "of" that add little SEO value. Removing them makes slugs shorter and more keyword-dense. For example, "the-best-way-to-learn-javascript" becomes "best-way-learn-javascript". This is optional — sometimes keeping stop words improves human readability.
No. All processing happens entirely in your browser using JavaScript. Your text never leaves your device and no data is stored or transmitted anywhere.
Accented and special characters (like é, ü, ñ, ç) are automatically transliterated to their ASCII equivalents (e, u, n, c) before slugifying, ensuring maximum URL compatibility across all platforms and browsers.
